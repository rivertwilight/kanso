## ü•∂ Cool Down Your Body

Traditional Chinese medicine believes that autumn is the season of "harvest," while winter is the season of "storage." During autumn and winter, sweat and semen should not be released excessively to allow for growth in the coming year. Therefore, exercise should focus on slight sweating rather than excessive sweating, and you can appropriately increase rest intervals during workouts or engage in lower-intensity training.

Among the four seasons, only summer requires **ample** sweating.

If you experience unformed stools after the weather cools down, it is likely due to insufficient yang energy in your body. Comparing the process of digesting food to cooking porridge, yang energy is like the fire that boils water. If the fire is insufficient, good porridge cannot be made.

There is a saying in folk wisdom: "Spring wraps and autumn freezes." Ancient wisdom is often very effective; the principle is that indoor temperatures are usually higher than outside. If you add clothes too early when entering a warm room, it can lead to sweating and harm yang energy. However, this should also be based on individual constitution, as it is not suitable for everyone.

However, one part of the body that should not be "cooled" is your feet. In fact, soaking your feet is a very good health-preserving method regardless of the season. There are many meridians in the feet, often referred to as the "second heart."

## üåô Sleep Early

Perhaps countless people have advised you to sleep early, but I must reiterate this point.

In autumn, the energy of heaven and earth converges, and the human body should also converge, hence the advice to **sleep early and rise early**. In winter, it is encouraged to **sleep early and rise late.** Observant readers may have noticed that this actually changes with the length of day and night.

You may have experienced this: feeling sleepy before eleven o'clock, but after staying up past eleven, you feel more awake. This is actually consuming energy for the next day in advance.

There is a saying: as long as you stay up late for a long time, it is not considered staying up late; it is merely changing your routine. In fact, the organs in the body have their own operating rhythms, just like nature, and will not change with your subjective alteration of your routine.

Some readers may encounter this situation: even though they sleep sufficiently, they still feel tired during the day. Possible reasons include:

- Chronic, consuming diseases
- Sleeping too late, going to bed in the middle of the night, which goes against the body's operating rhythm
- Poor sleep quality, frequent dreaming, easily waking up, or simply feeling that sleep is very shallow
- Neurasthenia
- If it is summer, it may also be due to excessive dampness, leading to a "heavy head," in which case you can consume foods that dispel dampness, such as yam and poria.

People often become reluctant to exercise in winter, and with shorter days and longer nights, it is easy to stay in bed.

An effective tip is to turn off electronic devices in advance, so the brain is not exposed to new stimuli and blue light.
