Every word in an interface is a design decision. Strip away the colors, the shadows, the border radii, and what's left is text. That text is doing most of the work.

A pixel-perfect layout with lazy copy feels broken. A plain layout with precise copy feels professional. The gap between the two is not aesthetics — it's trust.

## Button Labels

<DetailComparison
	answer="A"
	explanation={
		<>
			<p>
				A button label is a promise — it tells the user what will happen
				when they click. A simple test: a button label should complete
				the sentence "I want to ___." If it can't finish that sentence,
				it's not doing its job.
			</p>
			<p>
				"Help" is a noun — it names a category, not an action. "Get
				Help" is a verb phrase — it tells you what will happen. Same
				with "Desktop App" (what is it?) versus "Download Desktop App"
				(now I know what clicking does). "Become a Pro" assumes the user
				is "not" Pro; "Upgrade Plan" is concrete and actionable.
			</p>
		</>
	}

## Placeholder Text

Try typing in both inputs. Watch what happens to context as you go.

<DetailComparison
	answer="B"
	explanation={
		<>
			<p>
				Labels and placeholders have different jobs. One names the
				field. The other previews the format. Problems start when a
				single element tries to do both.
			</p>
			<p>
				When a placeholder is the only label, it vanishes the moment you
				start typing — you lose context mid-input. In a form with five
				fields, this means five moments of disorientation. A persistent
				label above the input keeps you oriented at all times, while the
				placeholder serves its proper role: showing the expected format,
				not naming the field. This distinction is also critical for
				accessibility — screen readers treat labels and placeholders
				differently.
			</p>
		</>
	}
>

<DetailComparison

## Empty States

<DetailComparison
	answer="B"
	explanation={
		<>
			<p>
				The user had intent — they typed a query, navigated somewhere
				with a purpose, and came up short. That moment of unmet
				expectation is the most overlooked in interface design. Most
				teams treat it as a non-moment. But it's actually the moment
				with the highest leverage over whether the user tries again or
				leaves.
			</p>
			<p>
				"No results found" is a dead end — a closed door with no
				signage. Echoing back the user's query ("No results for
				fluxcap") proves the system heard them correctly. Suggesting a
				next step ("Try a different keyword or check your spelling")
				opens a new door. Users who hit a helpful empty state try again.
				Users who hit a bare one leave.

## Changelogs

<DetailComparison
	answer="A"
	explanation={
		<>
			<p>
				A changelog is a retention marketing surface disguised as
				documentation. Every line is an opportunity to remind users why
				they pay — or an opportunity to sound like a git log nobody
				reads. The shift is simple: describe the outcome, not the
				change.
			</p>
			<p>
				The right reads like a git log — it describes what engineers
				did, using internal language ("support", "rendering issue",
				"bug"). The left speaks directly to the user: "You can now..."
				and "Your exports now include..." reframe every change as
				something the user gained. It also names the specific problem
				that was fixed ("blank pages on Firefox") instead of the vague
				"bug in PDF export". Users don't care about your bugs — they
				care about their workflows.

<DetailComparison

				When the product is slow, the copy <em>is</em> the product.
				Loading copy is the only moment where the interface has to
				justify its own existence in real time.
			</p>

				"Please wait..." is empty — it tells you nothing about what's

				action ("Generating your report"), sets an expectation ("about 6

## Answer Length in FAQ

<DetailComparison
	answer="B"
	explanation={
		<>
			<p>
				The length of a response is itself a message, separate from the
				words. A one-sentence answer feels confident. A three-paragraph
				answer feels like it's hiding something — or selling. "Can I
				cancel anytime?" is the most trust-sensitive question a user can
				ask.
			</p>
			<p>
				The left answer is technically accurate and thorough — but
				thoroughness is the problem. When someone asks "Can I cancel?",
				they want a yes or no. Three paragraphs of qualifications, edge
				cases, and support reassurances signal that the real answer is
				complicated. "Yes. No fees, no questions." is five words and
				total confidence. It answers the question, addresses the two
				anxieties behind it (cost and friction), and stops. Brevity is
				trust.
			</p>
		</>

## Notes

-   Interfaces should sound like a helpful colleague — clear, concise, and human.
-   Words compound. One well-written label is forgettable. A hundred of them make software feel _alive_.
