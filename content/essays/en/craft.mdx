# Craft

People use the word "craft" a lot. I've been trying to figure out what I actually mean by it.

The usual answers are either too thin or too romantic. Too thin: _attention to detail_. Too romantic: _love for the work_. Both are true. Neither is enough. A beautifully kerned button on a product no one needs is not craft. A founder working eighty-hour weeks on the wrong thing is not craft either.

So I sat with it for a while, and the shape I keep arriving at is this:

> **Craft = intent × care × form × taste**

Four things, all necessary, multiplied rather than added. If any one is zero, the whole collapses.

![What is Craft?](https://file.rene.wang/craft.png)

## The four

**Intent** is _why the thing exists._ The problem you fell in love with. The reason to make it at all. Without intent, the other three are aimed at nothing — beautiful answers to questions no one asked.

**Care** is _the relationship to the work over time._ Returning to a decision a week later to check if it still holds. Fixing the bug that only happens on Safari on iPad in landscape. Naming variables like they matter. Care is what shows up when no one is watching, and compounds over years.

**Form** is _the structure of the work._ Hierarchy, rhythm, spacing, the data model, the sentence logic, the skeleton underneath. Form is what a second person could inherit and extend without breaking the thing. It is load-bearing.

**Taste** is _the judgment in the work._ Among many structurally-sound options, which one is _right_. Inter or Söhne. This shadow or no shadow. Delete the section instead of fixing it. Taste is the hardest of the four because it is the least teachable — you can only develop it by exposure and reps.

---

## Why all four

It's tempting to reduce the list. But each pair leaves a characteristic failure mode, and you can feel them when you meet them.

**Intent + care, no form or taste** is the earnest amateur. The self-taught builder working every weekend on a real problem, shipping something shapeless and aesthetically off. Touching. Not craft yet.

**Intent + form, no care or taste** is the consultant's deliverable. The strategy deck that correctly diagnoses the problem, has a clean framework, and dies the moment it lands in the client's inbox. Correct. Dead.

**Form + taste, no intent or care** is the portfolio piece. The beautifully crafted Dribbble shot for an app that doesn't exist. The design system with no product behind it. Beautiful. Hollow.

**Taste + care, no intent or form** is the obsessive mood board. The years of Pinterest curation. The writer with beautiful sentences and no argument. The collector. Exquisite. Directionless.

Four flavors of almost-craft. Each one common. Each one a local maximum that feels like craft from the inside.

## A note on taste

Taste deserves its own paragraph because it's the one that gets faked the most.

Care and form can be systematized. You can write checklists. You can run reviews. You can ship a design system and enforce it. Taste resists all of that. It's the non-articulable part — the decision that isn't derivable from rules. You can't extract it, document it, or onboard someone into it directly.

Which is why taste is the clearest signal of a real craftsperson. The other three can be faked with discipline. Taste cannot.

---

## What this is for

I'm not trying to define craft so I can gatekeep it. I'm trying to define it so I can _notice_ where my own work is weak, and stop mistaking one axis for the whole.

When I look at a piece of my own work and something feels off — but the surface looks fine — the frame helps me locate it. Is the intent unclear? Is the form holding? Did I stop caring three weeks ago and just keep shipping? Did I pick the first good option instead of the right one?

It's a diagnostic, not a trophy.

> Craft is the only shape that fills all four at once. Everything else is a local maximum that almost feels like craft from the inside.

Less, but better.
