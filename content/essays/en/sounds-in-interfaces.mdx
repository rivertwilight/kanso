Most software is silent. We have spent a decade muting our keyboards, silencing our notifications, and turning every device to vibrate. Silence has become the default, and for good reason — most interface sounds were never worth hearing in the first place.

But silence is not the same as absence. The best interfaces use sound the way a good room uses light: sparingly, with intention, and only where it earns its place.

I want to look at two systems that take sound seriously, in very different ways. Then I want to try to articulate what separates a sound that belongs from a sound that intrudes.

---

## The Nintendo Switch is full of sound

Turn on a Switch and listen. The console wakes with a soft _click-pop_. The home menu greets you with a low chime that hangs in the air for a half-second longer than you expect. Move the cursor between game tiles and each one answers with a small wooden tap — different pitch, same family. Open a game and the tile expands with a satisfying _whoosh_. Close it and the sound reverses, like a door easing shut.

None of these sounds are decorative. Each one confirms a state change that you initiated. The cursor moved. The tile opened. The console heard you.

What makes the Switch's sound design remarkable is not that there is a lot of it — there is — but that it never feels like a lot. The sounds are quiet. They sit below speech volume. They are tuned to the same warm, slightly muffled palette, so that no single sound stands out as louder or sharper than the others. You stop noticing them as discrete events and start hearing them as the texture of the system.

This is closer to _kehai_ (気配) — the Japanese word for the faint sense of someone's presence — than to feedback in the traditional sense. The Switch is not announcing things to you. It is breathing alongside you.

The Joy-Con click when it seats into the rail is the same idea in physical form. It is louder than it needs to be for a mechanical latch, and it is tuned. Nintendo could have made it silent. They chose not to, because the click is the answer to a question you were about to ask: _is it in?_

## macOS is quieter, and more careful

Where the Switch fills its space with sound, macOS withholds. Boot the machine and you hear the startup chime — one of the most recognizable sounds in computing history — and then, for hours of work, almost nothing.

The system sounds that remain are tied to specific, irreversible, or system-level events. Empty the trash: a paper-crumple. Take a screenshot: a camera shutter. Drag a file off the dock: a _poof_. Receive a message: a single soft tone. Plug in power: a chime that confirms the machine is now drawing current.

Each of these sounds answers a question that the screen alone cannot answer well. The screenshot shutter confirms capture without forcing you to look at a flash. The power chime tells you the cable seated properly without you having to glance at the menu bar. The trash sound marks a destructive action — you cannot undo emptying the trash, and the sound acknowledges the weight of that.

[VIDEO: macOS system sounds — screenshot, trash, power chime]

What macOS _doesn't_ make sounds for is just as telling. Opening an app: silent. Switching windows: silent. Saving a file: silent. Typing: silent. The system reserves sound for moments when the user benefits from confirmation that the screen cannot easily provide — and stays out of the way the rest of the time.

The startup chime itself is worth pausing on. Jim Reekes designed it in 1991 to replace the harsh three-tone error sound that played when a Mac failed to boot. He wanted something that signaled _the machine is well_. He chose a C major chord, played on a custom palette, that resolves cleanly and decays slowly. It is the sound of a system that has finished checking itself and is ready to be trusted. Thirty-five years later, it still does that job.

---

## When sound earns its place

Looking at these two systems together, a few principles start to surface. They are not rules. They are the things that seem to be true of sounds that survive years of use without being muted.

**Sound should answer a question the screen cannot answer alone.** The Joy-Con click. The macOS power chime. The screenshot shutter. In each case, the user's attention is somewhere else — on the screen they are capturing, on the controller they are holding, on the cable they just plugged in. Sound reaches them where vision does not.

**Sound should be quieter than speech.** If a user has to lower their voice to talk over your interface, the interface is too loud. The Switch's menu sounds sit well below conversational volume. They are present without demanding presence.

**Sound should belong to a family.** The Switch's sounds share a timbre — warm, wooden, slightly soft-edged. macOS sounds share a different family — crisp, short, mechanical. Within a system, sounds should feel like they came from the same instrument. A user should never be startled by a sound that doesn't fit.

**Sound should mark weight.** Reversible actions deserve quiet sounds, or none at all. Irreversible actions — emptying trash, sending a message, completing a payment — deserve a sound that acknowledges what just happened. Apple Pay's two-tone confirmation is the canonical example: short, distinct, and impossible to mistake for anything else.

**Sound should never reward attention.** This is the line between feedback and gamification. A sound that confirms _you did the thing_ respects the user. A sound that celebrates _you did the thing, please do it again_ manipulates them. Slot machines, social apps, and language-learning streaks all use the second kind. Most of us mute them eventually.

**Most sounds shouldn't exist.** This is the principle that contains all the others. Every sound an interface makes is a small claim on the user's attention. The default should be silence, and each sound should have to argue its way past that default. The ones that survive — the chime, the click, the shutter — are the ones that had a real job to do.

---

Sound is one of those parts of an interface that you only notice when it is wrong. A keyboard that clacks when you wanted quiet. A notification that pierces a meeting. An app that congratulates you for tapping a button. The good ones disappear into the texture of using the thing, and you only realize they were there when you switch to something that doesn't have them.

Designing sound well is mostly the discipline of not designing most of it at all.
